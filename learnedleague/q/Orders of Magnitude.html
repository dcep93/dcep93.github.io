Smith's Blurb

Click to view blurb
Click to hide blurb
Hello and welcome to Orders of Magnitude! In this One-Day, you're going to be asked to come up with approximate counts for things of which there are, typically, a large number, across a variety of subjects. In general, the hope is that you might be able to come up with a reasonable estimate for most of these answers not by directly guessing them, but by analyzing their components or dimensions. (You may have heard these referred to as Fermi problems or by a number of other names.)

For example, suppose I asked you how many seats there were in all state legislatures combined in the US. You probably don't have a decent guess for that off the top of your head, but you probably know there are 50 states, and you'd probably suspect most of them have 2 houses in their legislature. You could then use other things you know (perhaps based on your experience as a voter in your own state or what you see in the news) to guess that an individual house in the legislature has member counts in the tens or hundreds. Suppose you guessed that on average an individual house had 100 members; you'd then probably guess around 10,000, and you wouldn't be too far off (per Wikipedia the current count is 7,383).

The great news about this quiz is that close enough is good enough! You literally only are guessing the nearest order of magnitude (i.e. the nearest power of ten), not the actual precise answer. Where things get a little complicated and you should definitely read the rest of the explanation below is how to express your answer to avoid making things complicated for scorers, and being on the same page of what constitutes the "nearest" order of magnitude.

First important clarification: please phrase your guesses as an order of magnitude/power of ten directly. Do not write your actual specific true guess. In other words, if you think the true precise answer to a question is 9,500, please guess 10,000. This is to avoid a nightmare for the One-Day scorers. All answers that are not a power of ten will be rejected, even if you somehow give the exact real answer. You may write your preferred power of ten in its everyday numerical form (1,000,000), or in exponential form (10^6 or 10e6).

Second important clarification: answers may be repeated. This whole thing is a multiple choice exercise as it is and I ultimately decided forcing each answer to be different would be less fun/potentially too easy.

Final important clarification: the correct power of ten will be the closest power of ten (higher or lower) on a lograthmic scale. For example, 10^5 will be the right order of magnitude if the true value is between 10^4.5 and 10^5.5. To avoid any confusion or awkward calculation, I've included a list of the first 20 powers of ten below and the range of numbers those values are closest to for our purposes. Also, to avoid regret/confusion, most answers are not very close to these boundaries anyway so you should mostly just focus on coming up with a good order of magnitude estimate.

10^2: 32 to 316
10^3: 317 to 3,162
10^4: 3,163 to 31,622
10^5: 31,623 to 316,227
10^6: 316,228 to 3,162,277
10^7: 3,162,278 to 31,622,776
10^8: 31,622,777 to 316,227,766

10^11: 31,622,776,602 to 316,227,766,016
